ANTIPLUG

Antiplug Effect: 

The primary side effect of long-term neural plug use. Affects a majority of mobile frame pilots to varying degrees of severity. Observed as a loss of personal drive and a psychological dependence on emotional, physical, and sexual submission, resulting from the repeated neural repathing involved in the use of the pilot’s brain as a CPU for piloting, targeting, and mission directive systems. In developed cases, affected pilots become permanently and wholly incapable of self-directed action.

The Antiplug Effect is not officially recognized by the Combined Earth Government’s Health Authority or Veteran Support Program.

 

Marina Kopacz woke from a deep, dreamless sleep.

She didn’t need to glance at her clock. 7:00AM, on the dot. Like every other morning. No alarm. One of her rules.

Without a moment of extra rest, despite the cold, she pulled herself upright and gazed, half-vacant, at her apartment window. Just barely visible through the narrow slits in her blinds, gentle snowfall declared the beginning of winter. 

She had never seen snowfall before. Lagrange-1 Delta, where she grew up, was an artificial colony with artificial weather, where it never snowed. Most of the combat she ever saw was in space, or else in Earth’s warmer climates. Tours around Lagrange-3, Lagrange-4, Far Luna, Central Africa, South America. Weatherless vacuums, artificially temperate colonies, deserts, former rainforests. The war never took her anywhere snowy.

The thought of the snow falling outside brought a little smile to her face. She’d told Jelena once, a few years ago, when she was just a researcher and not yet her Handler, that it was one of her dreams to see snow for herself.

Yet Marina did not go to open the blinds. She did not stand at the window and take in the view, nor did she run out into the cold air to feel the snow melt on her skin. No matter how much she knew, logically, that she would enjoy all of those things, that the snow appealed to the still-living romantic in her, that seeing it and touching it had always been one of her dreams — she hadn’t been told to do it, and therefore could not so much as want to.

 

 

Jelena felt like she was going to be sick. She always did, looking at Marina. She didn’t know exactly why, though she had a few guesses. Guilt, of course. Resentment, for sure. A fleck of jealousy, perhaps, towards the woman who seemed to feel neither.

She watched the former pilot go about her job, loading up their shitty van with box after box of whatever they were given, which they'd drive over to some other seedy warehouse in another city, a few hours over. The job was boring, unpleasant, poorly paid, definitely not entirely legal, and the best she could get that would keep them together, a commitment she wished every second of every day that she hadn’t made.

The worst part, as far as the former Handler was concerned, was that Marina looked fine, at least superficially. She smiled and laughed sincerely at Jelena’s jokes, ran circles around her in the games they played to pass the time, and she worked hard to boot. She aced every cognitive test she was given and was no slouch on the physicals, at least ‘till they stopped bothering with either. And she was still pretty, with her freckles and cute little buck-toothed smile, even with her skin breaking out and her hair a ragged mess. To anyone that saw her, she appeared to be in good health, and in reasonably good spirits for a retired soldier.

Those assessments were, invariably, dead fucking wrong. 

Marina was as deeply broken as a person could be. Jelena knew it better than anyone, because she watched and took notes while it happened.

 

“Mari. Pack these two next. Don’t put them on top of the others, they’re heavy and fragile.”

“Mm.”

She always just nodded and obeyed with a little glimmer in her dull eyes. 

God only knew what the others thought, to see Jelena standing around and smoking while poor Marina did all the work in this cold. It wasn’t just selfishness on her part, even if the guilt over it was the background radiation of her life at this point. This was good for Marina. Antiplugged pilots needed - needed, like water - firm guidance and clear, simple tasks. Jelena was just helping her to quench that thirst.

As often as she thought about up and leaving, finding something at least better than this for herself — she certainly could, with her qualifications — she just didn’t trust anybody else to take care of the poor girl. She sure as hell couldn’t take care of herself, and the Combined Earth Government would never lift a finger for her benefit; not only was she a former enemy combatant, but they were in even deeper denial about the Antiplug Effect than the Colony Union had been. They’d never give Marina the care she needed because there was nothing wrong with her, nothing wrong with neural plugs, nothing wrong with any of the pilots from either side of the war who came home with gaping holes in their souls. 

 

Marina came right to Jelena for instructions after she was done packing everything. She was smart enough to know what she should do next, but she had to hear it to do it. That was just how she worked now.

“Lock the back doors and wait in the passenger seat.”

“Mm.”

There it was again. Nod and obey. Jelena, ignoring the rise of bile in her throat that she had gotten so used to, flicked her cigarette into what little snow still remained from the morning.

It would’ve been better if the Union won the war. Maybe they wouldn’t have taken proper care of Marina either, but anything else would be better than being relocated off the colonies, to this backwater Earth city where their accents earned them glares and judgement. It drove Jelena mad to think about how different things could’ve been. Madder knowing that Marina, who had it worse than anyone, didn’t care. Couldn’t care. 

 

She settled into the driver’s seat and retrieved a ragged, dog-eared mapbook from the glovebox. Same destinations as usual today, but she liked to have it on her regardless.

Sometimes she wondered if, regardless of how the war went, the best result for both of them might have been if a C.E.G. Nanami ’s particle cannon had turned Marina and her Hollow Star to atoms and fond memories. Easier for Jelena, certainly, to not have to stare her regrets in the dead-eyed face every day. 

 

Alas, here they both were, mindbroken hound and heartbroken Handler, getting by on scraps under the Earth’s cold blue sky. 

It could be worse, she told herself. But she couldn’t imagine how.

 

 

“Out. You need to stretch your legs. Three laps around the station while we refuel.”

“Mm.”

Marina nodded, got out of the van, and started walking. Her legs were sore from all her bruises and tired from sitting for so long, so she appreciated the order to move them about a bit. She appreciated the order just for being an order.

The motor fueling station, the only evidence of human life in sight besides the long road it was situated by, was a strange quirk of life on Earth that Marina still found somewhat novel, even after growing up in the colonies where the only vehicles were small and electric. The smell, at least, wasn’t terribly unfamiliar. It wasn’t exactly unpleasant, but it reminded her of the hangars and shipboard mobile frame docking chutes she spent so much time in during the war, numb after every sortie with plug desync as her brain reconfigured itself as best as it could into its proper shape, just a little more of it missing each time.

The time and place were so different, so distant, but the smell was about the same.

 

Jelena seemed even more distracted than usual. Every time Marina passed her, she was gazing out beyond the horizon. It was obvious enough why. She felt bad for her old Handler, but couldn’t say or do anything about it without being asked to — and wouldn’t know what to say, anyway, fully conscious that this was mostly her own fault. Empty encouragement from the weight around Jelena’s neck wouldn’t do much to make her feel better.

By the end of her third lap, the van was refueled and Jelena was fishing around in her jacket pockets for some cash. She smiled weakly at Marina as she approached. A subtle slouch, bags under her eyes, shitty clothes that didn’t suit her, blonde crop of hair tousled and greasy. No makeup, no warmth in her eyes, no real sign of the old her. 

She used to be so perfect. So sharp, yet so gentle. Earth, and her burdens — Marina, most of all — had worn her down, made her dull and rough.

 

“Good job. Are you hungry?”

“A little.”

“I’ll get you a snack, then. Wait in the passenger seat for me.”

“Mm.”

When the joyful little sparkle in her head from following the order faded, Marina found herself staring idly at the surrounding barren landscape through the van windshield. Grey, arid. There used to be more plants, apparently, a couple centuries ago. They were all gone now. Only the hardiest shrubs survived.

She didn’t see herself as being much like those shrubs, robust little weeds that adapted specifically to thrive in these awful conditions. Rather, she was weak, hollow, achingly vulnerable, every moment of her life, ever since they put the socket in her neck and let the computer scrape out some part of her soul that she didn’t know she needed until it was gone. 

But she’d seen battle after battle of rockets and slugs and beams and heat blades and metal grinding violently against metal and survived, somehow. Maybe they had more in common than she knew.

 

 

The apartment was miserably icy-cold. They hadn’t been back long enough for the heating to kick in yet, but Jelena didn’t care, even as the poor broken girl before her stripped off every layer of her clothing. A jacket, a white shirt, a pair of jeans, plain black underwear, all old and ratty and ragged, came off and fell to the floor. With each piece, each relic of Marina’s old self that she removed, more of the purple and yellow bruises that marked her chill-flushed, freckled, pale skin revealed themselves.

The former pilot, bare and bruised and trembling, shivered and groaned at the cold air kissing her tender, blooming skin, but she didn’t complain. She wouldn’t have, even if she was capable. This wasn’t unpleasant at all: it was purpose for her. Her eyes positively glowed with it.

To Jelena, it was completely, utterly, thoroughly repulsive. 

 

“Sit.”

She obeyed with a fucking yip, mop of red hair falling messily in front of her face as she planted her ass on the floor and looked up with eager anticipation, knowing exactly what was coming next. She was genuinely happy. This was the only whole, true self she had left.

Not Marina. Not really. Not since a long, long time ago.

Hound. Mutt. 

 

Stupid. 

Good-for-nothing.

Useless.

Fucking. 

Dog.

 

Jelena stood. She towered over the repulsive thing. She took a handful of its hair in her fist and pulled it upwards, onto its shaking feet. It was fucking grinning. 

That was what disgusted her most of all. The thing in front of her looked like Marina, breathed like her, laughed and smiled like her. It still had her memories, and every mote of her intellect. But the only thing that could put the spark back in its eyes was this. Total submission. Pain. Purpose as a fucking punching bag.

“Stay standing. Get up if you fall.”

She started with a punch against its shoulder, in one of the few unmarked spots on its body. Not hard, but sudden enough that the thing couldn’t even brace itself. It fell to the floor immediately with a thud and a pained, thankful wheeze.

As it struggled to clamber to its feet, Jelena took to kicking it; in the shins, in the thighs, the hands, anywhere her boots connected. It yelped and cried with pain every time her sole hit a bruise, just like how Marina used to when the medics treated her combat injuries. That only spurred Jelena on.

 

One last kick had it on its stomach, still vainly trying to get up, arms quaking too much to lift itself even without any help from her boot.

This took every single standard for pilot care, for patient care, for basic decency, and snapped them all in half. It broke every rule in the book. Jelena knew that, because she wrote it. 

But damn, if it didn’t make her feel better.

“Stupid dog! Fucking get up!”

It struggled, legs buckling. Jelena lost patience with it, grabbing its hair again, yanking it up and shoving it face first into the wall. It moaned. When she turned it round to see its face, it looked her in the eyes and gave her a deep, depraved, masochistic laugh so revolting that it made Jelena’s head spin. That was a sound Marina never, ever made.

 

One to the stomach, just to make it stop.

Again, to the left shoulder, because she hated that this was the only thing that put the light back in its eyes.

Again, to the right thigh, because she hated that it didn’t care.

Again. Because she hated that it didn’t care.

Again, and again. Because it didn’t care.

Again, and again, and again. Because it didn’t care.

Again. Didn’t care about losing the war. Didn’t blink when the C.U. Hellehen went down, when they decommissioned the Hollow Star, or when the C.E.G. got boots on the ground in Lagrange-1 and massacred what was left of the C.U. government. Not a tear shed over losing the war that it signed away its life for, that stole so much more. Didn’t care.

Again. Didn’t care about losing its will. By the time anyone noticed it was too far gone to quit of its own accord, but the brass wouldn’t let anyone else discharge it. It was too good on the field. Not a hero, just a good tool. It knew that, intellectually. Would’ve been furious about being treated that way, on principle, when it could still act on those. But it didn’t resign. Hadn’t been told to. Too far gone. Didn’t care.

Again. Didn’t care that the C.E.G. was denying the existence of the Antiplug Effect. Didn’t care that this would keep happening to other pilots. Would-be heroes’ brains violated by unfeeling machines, neurons rewired to run targeting calcs and hold mission directives and run IFFs and follow kill orders, over and over and over again, till they couldn’t wire themselves back any more. Sent home like walking corpses, given fucking trauma care like they didn’t know exactly what was really wrong with them. Left to suffer and die, untreated. Forever and ever, for as long as neural plugs keep winning wars. Didn’t care.

Again. Didn’t care that Jelena was hurting it like this, twisting and breaking all her own rules for her own gratification. It knew all the guidelines she’d established when she was Handler Zega, when she thought she could still help the fucking thing. Firm guidance at all times. Simple tasks, given clearly. Orders. Straightforward, productive purpose to fill the hole in its brain, even for just a few minutes at a time. Gentle, constant care. Not up and out of bed at 7:00 every morning so it wasn’t the first thing she saw when she woke up. Not doing all the heavy lifting by itself. Not three laps around the fuel station just for some time alone. Not beatings and bruises left all over it. Not this. Never this. Didn’t care.

Again. Didn’t care that Jelena was hurting herself, too. She sobbed herself to sleep, full of guilt, every night. She hated herself for not helping it more, not insisting more, not finding some way to save it. She hated herself for giving in to it and hurting it like this. She hated it. She wished it had died. She told it as much, sometimes, when she was at her lowest. Didn’t care.

Again. Again. Again.

Didn’t care. Didn’t care. Didn’t care about anything but this. Just this. Just this sick fucking purpose.

 

With a final strike to her shoulder Marina fell to the floor again, completely limp, spark gone from her eyes, blood and drool dripping from her mouth. Only the occasional quaking of her body and the labored wheezing of her breath indicated that she was still alive.

 

Jelena stared, empty-eyed, just as deeply broken as her poor Mari — then dropped to her knees and burst into deep, shuddering sobs.
